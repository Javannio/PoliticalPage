Three Forces Reshaping Political Media

I

Audience Fragmentation

Primetime broadcast viewing dropped from 74.8% in 1990 to 43.7% by 2006. The "Big Three" networks lost half their audiences to hundreds of cable channels and the open web. Reaching a critical mass of voters through a single medium became nearly impossible.

II

Rising Advertising Costs

A 30-second spot on Grey's Anatomy in Minneapolis cost $20,000 in the 2006 cycle. Candidates were forced to pay rates averaging 65% above the federally mandated Lowest Unit Rate as stations steered them toward non-preemptible slots.

III

Media Saturation

The average American encounters 3,225 messages per day. Only 8% of evening viewers could name a product they'd just seen advertised — down from 34% in 1965. Standing out in this noise requires not just spending more, but spending radically smarter.

The Master Medium

The
Internet
Advantage

Gary Selnow of San Francisco State declared the Internet the "master medium" — combining text, audio, video, and direct mail capabilities in a single interactive, instantaneous platform unlike any before it.

Three Strategic Pillars

01 / Channel

Internet Advertising

Campaigns can purchase 1,000 targeted impressions for roughly $35 — far below network TV costs. Demotargeting and geotargeting allow ads to be served exclusively to, say, Republican women earning over $50,000 in swing states.

02 / Channel

E-mail Campaigns

Larry Purpuro of the RNC called e-mail "a tactical nuclear weapon in the future." Sending is virtually costless, and with proper customization it avoids the spam problem entirely — each message tailored to individual voter concerns.

03 / Channel

Candidate Websites

Visitors to a campaign site are self-selected engaged voters. Position papers, videos, speeches, and live feeds transform the website into a deep, constantly updated portal — far beyond what any 30-second spot can deliver.

02

Grabbing — and Keeping — Attention

A

Interactivity

Two-way communication transforms passive viewers into engaged participants. Candidates with high interactivity on their websites were perceived as more sensitive, responsive, and trustworthy — especially by previously apathetic voters.

The internet allows thousands of voters to interact with a campaign simultaneously — something no other medium can replicate at scale.

  • Online polls and live surveys
  • Chat rooms and candidate Q&A sessions
  • Webcasts and live campaign feeds
  • Volunteer sign-up and community meet-ups
  • Real-time donation drives via credit card
B

Customization

No other medium lets each individual voter choose what they want to learn. A well-designed site lets a voter concerned about healthcare dive deep into policy papers while a voter interested in foreign affairs explores those issues — on their own terms.

Technology enables "instant feedback" loops: tracking which ads drive click-throughs, which content fuels donations, and which pages hold attention — all without expensive external focus groups.

  • Personalized candidate "home pages" by interest
  • Geo- and demo-targeted ad delivery
  • Viral video distribution via e-mail
  • Real-time A/B testing of messages
  • Automated notification on new content in areas of interest

The Right Message.
Everywhere.

Network television is no longer the only answer. The fragmentation of the audience, the rising cost of advertising, and the saturation of modern media can be seen as negatives — or as the impetus for a smarter, more targeted future of political communication.

Whatever new technologies emerge, the fundamental truth remains: beneath it all is still the message and the candidate. No amount of digital wizardry can save a bad campaign — but the right message can now travel farther, faster, and more precisely than ever imagined.

"The right message — a compelling story that grabs the voters in just the right way — can now be sent to more people, more quickly and more effectively than ever before."
— Peter Fenn, Political Media Consultant & Author

Lead Story · Internet Strategy

The Internet Is Now the Campaign's Most Powerful Weapon — And Most Campaigns Are Still Ignoring It

As broadband penetration surpasses 45% of American homes, a new frontier of hyper-targeted, near-costless political communication sits largely untapped by the very campaigns that need it most.

A new study from the Pew Internet and American Life Project confirms what leading political strategists have argued for years: the Internet is no longer a supplementary channel. With 46% of online Americans seeking political news on the web — a figure that doubled since 2002 — campaigns failing to invest in digital infrastructure are, as one consultant puts it, "fighting a modern war with muskets."

From 2002 to 2006, the share of American adults with high-speed internet grew by an extraordinary 265% — from 17% to 45%. Primetime broadcast viewership meanwhile shed 30 points in a decade, dropping from 74.8% in 1990 to under 44%.

"As a campaigning tool, e-mail is going to be what I would consider a tactical nuclear weapon in the future." — Larry Purpuro, RNC

The cost contrast is stark. A single 30-second prime drama spot in the 2006 cycle cost over $20,000 in mid-size markets. The same campaign can purchase 1,000 precisely targeted online impressions for $35. "The math," as Peter Fenn observes, "is not subtle."

E-mail

Building the List: Why E-mail Addresses Are the New Gold

Both parties collected hundreds of thousands of addresses in 2000. Strategists say those lists — grown over successive cycles — now represent a communication asset that will ultimately outpace broadcast television. "Only the absence of a phone book for the Internet is stopping it from happening already," writes Dick Morris.

Targeting

Demotargeting: Precision Voter Ads at Fraction of TV Cost

New demographic targeting lets campaigns serve ads exclusively to defined voter segments — by party affiliation, income, gender, and ZIP code simultaneously. What previously required a massive multi-channel TV buy can now be executed through a single digital platform for pennies on the dollar.

Websites

Candidate Portals: From Brochure to Living Command Center

Research confirms high interactivity on candidate websites directly increases perceived trustworthiness. Campaigns investing in polls, chat interfaces, live webcasts and constantly refreshed content turn websites into genuine voter-engagement engines — not digital billboards.

Strategy Feature

Howard Dean's MeetUp Strategy — How One Candidate Invented the Modern Digital Campaign

In 2004, former Vermont governor Howard Dean used MeetUp.com to form an online community of supporters who gathered in person nationwide. The approach — creating a real-world community rooted in online organisation — had never been done before in presidential politics. It is now accepted strategy. The Internet's ability to bring supporters together, coordinate volunteer activities, spread messaging organically, and most importantly collect donations at scale via credit card from donors who might never have given otherwise — proved transformative. Dean's campaign raised millions online before the primaries, setting a template Barack Obama would perfect in 2008.

Interactivity

Two-Way Communication Transforms Passive Viewers Into Invested Participants

Research by Lynda Lee Kaid (2002) found that candidates with high website interactivity were perceived as more sensitive and trustworthy — particularly by apathetic voters who are hardest to reach. Online polls, candidate Q&A, chat rooms, and live webcasts create the sense that a voter has a genuine stake in the campaign's outcome.

  • → Live polls and real-time surveys
  • → Webcasts and virtual town halls
  • → Volunteer coordination at scale
  • → Credit-card fundraising portals
Customisation

No Other Medium Lets Each Voter Build Their Own Information Experience

A well-designed candidate website lets a voter worried about healthcare dive deep into policy papers, while one focused on foreign affairs explores those issues — both on their own terms, without friction. Technology enables "instant feedback" loops: tracking which ads drive click-throughs, which content fuels donations, and which pages hold attention — without expensive focus groups.

Opinion · Peter Fenn

The Right Message Can Now Travel Farther, Faster, and Cheaper Than at Any Point in History

The three forces reshaping modern campaigns — audience fragmentation, rising TV costs, and media saturation — are not a crisis. They are the greatest opportunity political media has ever offered.

Cable let us target. The Internet lets us converse. E-mail lets us reach individuals, not demographics. The campaigns that thrive will not be those with the biggest television budgets, but those with the most sophisticated digital infrastructure and the courage to use it. The tools are here. The audiences are online. The cost is negligible. What is missing is the will.

"Right now we consultants drop the piano on the mouse. The future holds more in store." — Republican strategist Mike Murphy
Money & Media

Why the Internet Will Not Replace TV — But Will Force It to Improve

Internet banner ads, targeted cable buys, and e-mail campaigns do not replace television. They force the medium to become sharper. Campaigns that combine disciplined digital targeting with refined TV storytelling will outperform those that rely on either alone.

By announcing her presidential candidacy on her own website, Senator Clinton signalled that the web is now a primary venue for the most consequential political communications — not a footnote.
